How Forestry Mulching Improves Deer Habitat

A practical guide for rural landowners, hunters and property managers in Western Wisconsin and Eastern Minnesota.

Why Habitat Improvement Matters

Overgrown woods often block sunlight from reaching the forest floor. When that happens, native grasses, forbs and browse plants begin to disappear. Selective clearing and forestry mulching can open portions of the woods back up so desirable vegetation can grow again.

This type of work can improve food sources, cover and movement for deer, turkey and other wildlife. It can also make your property easier to access and manage.

Simple Bedding Area Concept

What Forestry Mulching Can Help Improve

  • Sunlight reaching the forest floor
  • Native browse and regeneration
  • Bedding cover and edge habitat
  • Travel corridors through thick woods
  • Access for landowners, stands and habitat management
  • Opening areas for future food plots or screening cover

Food Plot Access and Preparation

Forestry mulching is also useful for preparing future food plot areas and improving access to them. On many properties, overgrown brush and invasive species make it difficult to reach existing plots or open new ones in good locations.

Clearing brush, widening UTV trails and opening small pockets of sunlight can make a property easier to manage. That can help landowners build better trail systems, reach stands more quietly and maintain food plots with less hassle.

About Bigger Bucks

No habitat work can guarantee bigger bucks. Deer age structure, neighborhood pressure, surrounding habitat, nutrition and genetics all play a role. But better habitat can create conditions that encourage more consistent deer use, healthier browse and better cover, which is why so many landowners improve habitat as part of a long-term plan.
